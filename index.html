// MISSION VOCABULARY · TAP TO REVEAL
Six terms. You'll use every one of them when you defend a design decision. If you can't use a word in a sentence, you don't know it yet.
AERODYNAMIC
Shaped to move through air with minimum resistance. A nose cone is aerodynamic. A flat board is not.
PAYLOAD
The cargo a rocket carries. The reason the rocket exists. Everything else on your rocket is there to deliver this safely.
FAIRING
A smooth structural shell that protects what's underneath and reduces drag. On your rocket, the 3D-printed collar that holds the fins.
APOGEE
The highest point a rocket reaches before falling back. Maximum altitude. The number you're trying to make bigger.
DRAG
The force of air pushing back against the rocket as it flies. More drag means lower apogee. Shape, surface, and size all change drag.
STABILITY
A rocket's tendency to fly straight instead of tumbling. Comes from fin placement, weight distribution, and balance. Without it, you don't have a rocket — you have shrapnel.

// CARGO MANIFEST · WHAT THE COLONY NEEDS
THE PAYLOAD
// FOUR ITEMS · ONE BOTTLE · ONE SHOT

A colony's life support is failing. They need four specific things. You can't substitute. You can't skip one. You build all four — represented in craft materials — and they all have to fit inside the cut 2-liter bottle. And they all have to survive impact when the rocket lands.

  • SCRUBBERS
    CO₂ scrubbers. So they can keep breathing what they exhale. Apollo 13 made this with duct tape and a sock. You have pipe cleaners.
  • WATER
    Water purifier. So they can drink. Build something that could filter water if it were real.
  • OXYGEN
    O₂ canister. For the immediate emergency. Pressurized in real life — represent it however you want.
  • SEEDS
    Seed packet. So the colony can keep going after we're gone. Watney grew potatoes on Mars. Same idea.
  • FIT
    All four items must fit inside the cut 2-liter bottle together. Nothing sticks out. Nothing rattles loose.
  • DROP TEST
    Loaded payload survives a drop from standing height onto a hard floor. If it doesn't, redesign the protection. The cargo manifest doesn't change.

// PART SPEC · NOSE CONE
NOSE CONE
// SITS ON TOP · CUTS THE AIR · HOLDS THE BALLAST
The nose cone sits on top of the upper bottle. Its job is two things: (1) reduce drag on the way up, and (2) hold ballast at the front of the rocket so it flies straight on the way down. Without nose-end mass, your rocket tumbles. Without a smart shape, your apogee suffers. Both matter.
  • BASE OUTER ⌀~110 mm (must match top of upper bottle)
  • TOTAL HEIGHT≤ 140 mm (printer ceiling)
  • BALLAST CAVITY22 mm Ø × 40 mm deep cylinder — REQUIRED, in the base
  • SHAPEyour call — conical, ogive, parabolic, blunt — defend it
  • WALL THICKNESS≥ 2 mm (don't print eggshell)
  • BASE INTERFACEflat or matched to bottle top — must seat cleanly
Tinkercad technique: design your cone shape, then place a 22 × 22 × 40 mm cylinder set to Hole at the base. Group the solid + hole together. The hole stays as a cylindrical cavity. Test it — does the cavity reach 40 mm deep into the cone? If your cone is shorter than 40 mm, you have a problem.
// MASS TARGET · WHY YOUR ROCKET FLIES STRAIGHT
NOSE-END MASS
// PRINTED CONE + WASHERS LOADED IN CAVITY
A rocket flies stably when its center of gravity is forward of its center of pressure. Translation: the nose end must be heavier than the tail end. The ballast cavity exists for this reason. After your cone prints, you load steel washers into the 22 × 40 cavity until the total nose-end mass hits the target.
  • TOTAL TARGET120–160 g (cone + washers combined)
  • VERIFICATIONweighed on digital scale at print pickup
  • PRINT MASSyour call — pick infill to balance speed and weight
  • BALLASTsteel washers, fill the cavity until in range
  • BELOW 120 grocket tumbles on descent — bad data
  • ABOVE 160 gapogee suffers — also bad data
// PART SPEC · FAIRING
FAIRING
// CYLINDRICAL COLLAR · WIDEST PART OF BOTTLE · HOLDS THE FINS
The fairing is a 3D-printed cylinder that wraps around the widest part of the bottle — the lower body, NOT the nozzle. The fins (designed in Cuttle later this week) attach to it. The nozzle must stay completely clear — that's where the launcher mounts. The fairing can extend downward toward the nozzle but cannot block access to it.
  • SHAPEcylinder (no taper, no transition)
  • INNER ⌀~112 mm (110 mm bottle + 1 mm foam tape buffer on each side)
  • HEIGHT40–60 mm typical
  • WALL THICKNESS≥ 3 mm (this part takes load)
  • FIN ATTACHMENT3 or 4 flat surfaces, evenly spaced on outside
  • FIN SLOTSplan now even if you cut fins later
  • NOZZLE CLEARANCEdo NOT block bottle nozzle/threads — launcher needs access
Pre-assembly required: 1 mm foam tape gets wrapped around the bottle where the fairing seats — this prevents the PLA from cracking when the bottle expands under launch pressure. Plan your inner diameter for it. The foam comes from your teacher.
